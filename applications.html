Collaborative Robotics

Manufacturing

The contemporary manufacturing landscape is characterized by intricate electro-mechanical systems, making today's production plants complex. This complexity is not just inherent but also grows across the system's life cycle phases, such as design, development, and operation. In response to this, advancements in sensing technologies, virtualization, and computing power have given rise to the concept of digital twins. A digital twin (DT) is a real-time digital representation of a physical system that facilitates testing and validation [5].

Given the potential of DTs to manage system complexity across various scientific domains, there has been a significant push to harness them in addressing the manufacturing challenges created by the need for decentralized and customization production controls. To meet these challenges, there is a growing emphasis on the interplay between humans and machines, with human-robot collaboration (HRC) using cobots emerging as a pivotal aspect. HRC seeks to blend the unique strengths of humans and robots—like dexterity, intelligence, and sensing—to accomplish tasks such as assembly processes [5]. From a design viewpoint, HRC embodies a multifaceted biomechatronic structure, with design decisions heavily reliant on computer-aided simulation models for validation and failure analysis.

Bilberg et al (2019) delved into the advantages of DTs for HRC, emphasizing skills distribution between humans and robots, optimizing robot trajectories, workload balancing, and robot program generation, especially in variant-oriented production contexts. The system consists of four modules as shown in Figure 1.

  • Task Decomposition Engine

    This module dissects product assembly into various tasks, evaluating them based on physical attributes and assembly traits. Tasks are scored for their automation suitability, categorizing them as manual or robotic.

  • Assembly Task Library

    Comprising function-blocks representing assembly tasks, it connects to an event-based human and robot simulation mirroring the actual production system. The simulation, with predefined key positions, generates operational plans and estimated cycle times, facilitating efficient robot program development.

  • Decision Engine

    Receiving simulation data, this module allocates resources for balanced production scheduling and crafts a control program.

  • Control Interface

    This module executes the robot program and communicates task instructions to the operator via an interactive display.

By leveraging the power of digital twins in human-robot collaboration, modern manufacturing can address its complexities and drive productivity to new heights.

Healthcare

As the global population ages, a spike in life expectancy brings forth three prominent challenges: a surge in the elderly demographic, escalating cognitive and physical challenges among them, and a rise in single-person households. There is an escalating need for effective elderly care solutions, such as healthcare service robots and assistive technologies.

Ribeiro et al (2021) introduced CHARMIE (Collaborative Healthcare/Home Assistant Robot by Minho Industrial Electronics) [6]. This anthropomorphic robot, as shown in Figure 2, is tailored for both healthcare and domestic environments. Unlike other robots, CHARMIE is unique in its machine learning capabilities, allowing it to learn and refine tasks via direct interaction and observation. CHARMIE boasts a suite of functionalities: dynamic obstacle detection, safe navigation, human-robot interaction systems, and domestic object manipulation [6]. The robot handles tasks in nursing homes, homes, and healthcare facilities, ranging from fall detection to setting up tables. CHARMIE’s design promises a future where the elderly can lead more independent, healthier lives. Examples of its adaptability include delivering food trays in hospitals, transporting medicine in healthcare facilities, and assisting in household chores [6].

Its versatility is showcased through tasks like:

  • Goods transportation and pick and place tasks.
  • Following and communication with users through speech and visual cues.
  • Navigating and recognizing objects in its environment.
  • Assisting users in tasks they might find challenging on their own.

Education

Higher education institutions are increasingly incorporating active learning with elements of group discussion. The goal is to foster problem identification and resolution skills, preparing students for the global workforce. Traditional education modes are evolving, where learners initially use an e-Learning platform like MOOC for content comprehension and subsequently engage in classroom group discussions to apply their knowledge. This e-Learning paradigm is also witnessing the integration of cobots to enhance the learning experience. The development of social, autonomous robots capable of human-like interactions including gestures, body language, facial expressions, and touch hold promise for the education sector.

The study by Tojo et al presents a collaborative e-Learning system where the entire learning process occurs in a remote environment, accessed by learners via the Internet. The challenge lies in ensuring a realistic learning experience and fostering engagement during group discussions. A collaborative robot named Interactive Tutor Robot (ITR) was introduced to enhance interactions. The primary focus was to analyze the impact of the robot's gestures on learners, evaluated through experimental observations and semantic differential (SD) assessments.

The e-Learning environment, as shown in Figure 3, consists of an ITR and a notebook computer connected to the Internet. The computer shows the e-Learning content, while the ITR facilitates the learning process. Individual learning can be distinguished with a single unit as 1st-level individual learning and group learning with multiple units as 2nd-level group learning [7]. The latter is referred to as a collaborative e-Learning system for distinction.

In this model:

  • Interaction is between the ITR and student, where learning support information is exchanged and understanding gauged.
  • Communication occurs between multiple ITRs, sharing and accumulating learning support data.
  • Collaborative learning happens between learners. In the 1st-level, it's mostly individual study, while in the 2nd-level, learners collaborate, applying knowledge, sharing insights, and refining skills. Unlike traditional group learning where learners congregate, in our system, they use ITRs as avatars for virtual group learning.

The communication mechanism involves:

  • From ITR to Learner: Sounds and sound effects, and visual gestures facilitated by robot actuators for movement and audio functionality.
  • From Learner to ITR: Visual cues like facial expressions, voice, and tactile feedback by touching the robot. The ITR is equipped with cameras for sight, microphones for sound, and touch sensors for tactile feedback.

In conclusion, the fusion of active learning and collaborative robots in e-Learning systems offers a promising avenue for enhanced, interactive, and globally accessible education.
