Collaborative Robotics

Overview

Collaborative robots, commonly referred to as Cobots, are a unique segment of robotic automation primarily designed for safety. Unlike their industrial robot counterparts, they boast rounded edges, force limitations, and lightweight designs [3]. These robots use an array of sensors that help them avoid collisions with humans. In the event of unexpected contact, safety protocols ensure that the cobots are promptly shut down.

These robots are not just built for safety but are also developed to operate seamlessly alongside human workers within a shared workspace. This is due to advancements in touch technology, artificial intelligence, mobile technology, computer vision, and cognitive computing. These innovations enable these relatively small and less power-intensive robots to be fully aware of their environment. They can safely perform various tasks close to humans and can also rapidly acquire new tasks through techniques like demonstration and reinforcement learning.

Typically, the division of labor sees cobots handling repetitive and mundane tasks, leaving more intricate and mentally demanding jobs to their human counterparts. Such an arrangement is beneficial for both. While the cobot ensures accuracy, uptime, and repeatability, the human worker offers intelligence and superior problem-solving capabilities [3].

The realm of possible applications for these cobots is vast, ranging from manufacturing-related functions like assembly line support, packaging automation, and product quality control to sectors like healthcare and education. As industries begin to recognize the economic advantages of integrating cobots, the market for them is poised for substantial growth. This growth is further propelled by the current shortage of skilled labor and the escalating costs associated with human workers [3].

Cobot Design Principles

Designing cobots involves creating a collaboration between robots and humans. This gives the cobots an understanding of their environment and equips them with actions that achieve a collaborative goal. Zaatari et al (2019) state the main components of a cobot design:

  1. Learning: Cobots learn skills in a manner akin to humans, utilizing trial and error, observation, feedback, and posing questions [4].
    • Offline: The programmer designs the learning algorithm and offers initial data, which could be demonstrations or training sets, for the cobot to learn from.
    • Online: The operator can directly influence the cobot's learning by providing feedback, personalized demonstrations, or answers to the robot's queries. Additionally, the operator can indirectly affect the cobot's learning by being a part of its observed environment.
  2. Communication: Communication can be both verbal (speech) or non-verbal, including gestures as shown in Figure 1, gaze, haptics, head pose, and user interfaces [4].
    • Offline: The programmer sets up and defines potential cobot actions and the fundamental motion control. This is referred to as the Traditional role.
    • Online: The operator explicitly communicates with the cobot in real-time, directing it towards predefined actions.
  3. Optimization: The optimization aims to minimize factors like operator workload, energy consumption, and time, while maximizing elements like comfort, trust, and product quality. This method often results in better performance compared to human-only operation [4].
    • Offline: The programmer crafts cost functions and optimization algorithms. They model essential aspects of the cobot's environment, like obstacles and tool positions, based on the robot's actions.
    • Online: During operation, the cobot's performance is implicitly influenced by the operator, who becomes part of its cost function.

In contrast to traditional industrial robot programming, where a robot functions deterministically and without human-awareness, Human-Robot Collaboration (HRC) introduces unpredictability due to human involvement. This dynamic requires cobots to adapt and respond in real-time, making the above features critical for effective collaboration.
