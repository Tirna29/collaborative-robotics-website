Collaborative Robotics

Advantages of Cobots

Cobots are increasingly becoming the preferred choice over traditional industrial robots due to the multitude of benefits they offer. From ensuring safety to delivering rapid returns on investment, cobots cater to diverse needs in various environments. Some of the advantages of cobots are:

  1. Cost-Efficient Deployment

    Cobots present a cost-effective automation solution. Their user-friendly programming reduces integration time and resources. Their in-built safety measures eliminate the need for additional protective equipment like fences. This reduction in auxiliary costs, coupled with their innate ability to enhance production processes, translates to an improved bottom line, making cobots an attractive proposition for a broader range of customers.

  2. Safety

    Collaborative robots prioritize the safety of their human coworkers. Equipped with advanced features such as sensors to detect and avoid collisions, force limitations, and sleek designs, they minimize workplace accidents and injuries. Additionally, cobots have overcurrent protections and passive compliance to handle unexpected contacts. By taking on hazardous and repetitive tasks, like welding, soldering, chemical handling, foundry work, and heavy lifting, cobots prevent human workers from being exposed to potential risks. This results in heightened productivity and reduces operational expenses [1].

  3. Consistent Accuracy

    Operating with uniform force and consistency, cobots ensure that products are of unmatched quality, perfectly aligned, and accurate.

  4. Enhanced Productivity

    Assigning repetitive, monotonous, and potentially dangerous tasks to cobots accelerates their completion, leading to enhanced productivity levels. When compared with industrial robots, collaborative robots often emerge as the more economically viable and efficient choice, especially when harnessed for suitable applications [2].

  5. Flexibility

    Cobots are renowned for their adaptability as individuals with no prior experience in robot programming can instruct them. In some scenarios, guiding the robot arm physically to desired locations is all it takes to teach the machine. This adaptability allows cobots to juggle multiple tasks and switch between them seamlessly. With the aid of human-machine interfaces on mobile tablets, human workers can swiftly reprogram cobots, reducing downtime and increasing adaptability.

  6. Quick Return on Investment (ROI)

    The financial viability of cobots is evident in their swift ROI. Their lower upfront costs, capability to automate a multitude of tasks, and substantial contributions to productivity make them a lucrative investment [2]. Most users witness a reliable ROI within a few months, making cobots a dependable choice for those cautious about their automation investments.

Limitations of Cobots

While cobots, offer numerous advantages in various applications, they also come with certain limitations:

  1. Dependency on Human Supervision

    Although cobots can operate continuously in theory, they still require human intervention or oversight. For instance, when the factory is vacant at night, the cobot may still need supervision. In contrast, industrial robots can function at full throttle without human presence [1].

  2. Payload Restrictions

    Cobots are generally designed for light-duty tasks. Typically, they handle payloads ranging from 3 to 10 kg, with some models capable of managing up to 35 kg [1]. In contrast, certain six-axis robots can manage up to 2 tons depending on the application. It's important to note that cobots are not suitable for heavy-duty tasks. Therefore, for higher loads, cobots might not be the best choice.

  3. Safety Approval Challenges

    Securing safety approval for cobots can be a complex process. There are numerous safety regulations to consider, and modifications in the cobot's tasks or tools might necessitate renewed certifications. For example, in the European Union, cobots must be accredited by a notifying body as part of the CE-marking process, ensuring compliance with EU standards [2]. Changes can alter the cobot's initial purpose which could demand a new safety certification, leading to additional expenses and delays.

  4. Speed Constraints

    Safety is paramount for cobots, and this often comes at the expense of speed. Most cobots move at an average speed of 250 millimeters per second, which is four times slower than traditional industrial robots [1]. When human users engage with cobots, their speed drops further to ensure safety, leading to longer cycle times. Consequently, high-speed applications are not ideal for cobots.

Structural design Analysis

Cobots can be categorized based on their structural design and the number of degrees of freedom (DoF), which varies from one to fourteen. Higher DoF enhances pose versatility. The primary classification based on DoF are Anthropomorphic, Cartesian, SCARA, and Torso. Anthropomorphic cobots, which form 90% of the market, consist of a serially connected mechanical structure with at least four joints. Torso cobots mimic human movements, boasting high versatility with twisting, bending, and rotating capabilities. Cartesian cobots are grounded on an orthogonal Cartesian system, while SCARA cobots feature two horizontally mobile arms ending in a prismatic coupling for vertical movements.

Accuracy

Accuracy indicates the divergence between the targeted and achieved poses. Over 90% of anthropomorphic cobots demonstrate a precision between 0.01 mm and 0.20 mm, with payload capacities ranging from 0.3 kg to 20.0 kg.
